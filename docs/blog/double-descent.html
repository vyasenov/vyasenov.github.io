Double Descent Without the Neural Networks

parametric models
statistical inference
machine learning
Published

June 22, 2026

9 min read

Background

Every statistician learns a reflex early: a model that fits the training data perfectly — zero residuals, zero training error — has almost certainly overfit, and will predict badly out of sample. The bias–variance trade-off draws a tidy U: too simple and you are biased, too flexible and variance explodes, so you aim for the sweet spot in between. Interpolation is the cardinal sin.

Modern machine learning violates this reflex daily and gets away with it. Enormous neural networks that interpolate their training data — driving training error to exactly zero — routinely generalize beautifully. The phenomenon was dramatized under the name double descent by Belkin et al. (2019): push model complexity past the interpolation point and out-of-sample error, after spiking, comes back down again, sometimes below the classical sweet spot. The bias–variance U is only the first half of the story.

It is tempting to file this under “deep learning is weird.” I think that is exactly the wrong lesson, and the cleanest correction comes from Hastie, Montanari, Rosset, and Tibshirani (2022), who show that the entire phenomenon appears in the most classical model we have — linear least squares. No networks, no kernels, no mystery. My goal here is to read double descent the way a statistician should: as a story about interpolation, the geometry of the \(p > n\) regime, and — above all — about what happens when you forget to regularize.

Notation

Take the usual linear model with i.i.d. data,

\[y_i = x_i^\top \beta + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \mathbb{E}(\varepsilon_i) = 0, \quad \text{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma^2,\]

with \(x_i \in \mathbb{R}^p\) and a design matrix \(X\) that is \(n \times p\). The single quantity that organizes everything is the overparametrization ratio

\[\gamma = p / n.\]

When \(\gamma < 1\) the problem is underparametrized (more rows than columns, ordinary least squares applies); when \(\gamma > 1\) it is overparametrized and least squares no longer has a unique solution. In that regime the natural estimator is the minimum-norm, or ridgeless, least squares solution

\[\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^+ X^\top y = X^+ y,\]

the least-squares fit of smallest Euclidean norm, and equivalently the limit of ridge regression as the penalty vanishes, \(\hat\beta = \lim_{\lambda \to 0} \hat\beta_\lambda\). When \(p > n\) it interpolates: \(x_i^\top \hat\beta = y_i\) for every training point. Write the signal-to-noise ratio as \(\text{SNR} = \|\beta\|_2^2 / \sigma^2 = r^2/\sigma^2\), and measure performance by the out-of-sample prediction risk at a fresh test point, \(R = \mathbb{E}\big[(x_0^\top \hat\beta - x_0^\top\beta)^2\big] = \|\hat\beta - \beta\|_\Sigma^2\), where \(\Sigma = \text{Cov}(x_0)\).

A Closer Look

The curve

Here is the whole phenomenon in one picture. Fix \(n\), let \(p\) sweep from small to large, fit ridgeless least squares at each step, and plot the out-of-sample risk against \(\gamma\). The code below does exactly that in the isotropic case \(\Sigma = I\), holding the signal norm \(\|\beta\|^2\) fixed so the comparison across \(\gamma\) is fair.

  • R
  • Python
set.seed(1988)
library(MASS)   # for ginv(); install.packages("MASS")

n      <- 100              # training sample size
sigma2 <- 1                # noise variance
r2     <- 5 * sigma2       # squared signal norm => SNR = 5
reps   <- 50

gammas <- c(seq(0.10, 0.95, by = 0.05), seq(1.05, 6, by = 0.25))
risk   <- numeric(length(gammas))

for (g in seq_along(gammas)) {
  p  <- round(gammas[g] * n)
  rr <- numeric(reps)
  for (b in 1:reps) {
    beta <- rnorm(p); beta <- beta / sqrt(sum(beta^2)) * sqrt(r2)  # ||beta||^2 = r2
    X    <- matrix(rnorm(n * p), n, p)
    y    <- X %*% beta + rnorm(n, sd = sqrt(sigma2))
    bhat <- ginv(X) %*% y                  # min-norm (ridgeless) solution: X^+ y
    rr[b] <- sum((bhat - beta)^2)          # isotropic risk = ||bhat - beta||^2
  }
  risk[g] <- mean(rr)
}

plot(gammas, risk, type = "b", pch = 19, log = "y",
     xlab = expression(gamma == p/n), ylab = "out-of-sample risk",
     main = "Double descent in ridgeless least squares")
abline(v = 1, lty = 2)                     # interpolation boundary
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(1988)

n      = 100              # training sample size
sigma2 = 1.0              # noise variance
r2     = 5 * sigma2       # squared signal norm => SNR = 5
reps   = 50

gammas = np.r_[np.arange(0.10, 1.0, 0.05), np.arange(1.05, 6.01, 0.25)]
risk   = np.zeros(len(gammas))

for g, gam in enumerate(gammas):
    p  = max(1, round(gam * n))
    rr = np.zeros(reps)
    for b in range(reps):
        beta = np.random.normal(size=p)
        beta = beta / np.linalg.norm(beta) * np.sqrt(r2)   # ||beta||^2 = r2
        X    = np.random.normal(size=(n, p))
        y    = X @ beta + np.random.normal(scale=np.sqrt(sigma2), size=n)
        bhat = np.linalg.pinv(X) @ y                       # min-norm solution: X^+ y
        rr[b] = np.sum((bhat - beta)**2)                   # isotropic risk
    risk[g] = rr.mean()

plt.semilogy(gammas, risk, "o-")
plt.axvline(1.0, ls="--", color="gray")                    # interpolation boundary
plt.xlabel(r"$\gamma = p/n$"); plt.ylabel("out-of-sample risk")
plt.title("Double descent in ridgeless least squares")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

The curve climbs as \(\gamma \to 1\) from below, blows up at the interpolation boundary, then descends a second time for \(\gamma > 1\) before flattening out. In the isotropic limit Hastie et al. (2022) make this exact: as \(n, p \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to \gamma\),

\[ R \to \begin{cases} \sigma^2 \dfrac{\gamma}{1 - \gamma}, & \gamma < 1, \\[2ex] \underbrace{r^2\Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{\gamma}\Big)}_{\text{bias}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2 \dfrac{1}{\gamma - 1}}_{\text{variance}}, & \gamma > 1. \end{cases} \]

Below the boundary the risk is pure variance — no bias at all — and it diverges as \(\gamma \to 1\). Above it, the risk is the sum of a bias term that grows with \(\gamma\) and a variance term that shrinks with \(\gamma\). That tension is the engine of the second descent.

Bias up, variance down

Why does the risk fall again past \(\gamma = 1\)? The two components move in opposite directions, and understanding why is the heart of the matter.

The bias rises with \(\gamma\) for a reason that should be familiar if you have read about the \(p>n\) regime: the minimum-norm solution is confined to the row space of \(X\), an \(n\)-dimensional subspace sitting inside \(\mathbb{R}^p\). As \(p\) grows, that row space accounts for an ever-smaller slice of the ambient coefficient space, so the part of \(\beta\) the data can never see — and which the min-norm rule simply zeroes out — grows with it. More parameters, more of \(\beta\) left on the table, more bias.

The variance falls with \(\gamma\), which feels backwards: surely more parameters means more variance? Not for the minimum-norm interpolator. The intuition is in the name. Adding columns to \(X\) gives the solver more directions over which to spread the fit, so the minimum-norm solution to \(Xb = y\) generally has smaller norm as \(p\) grows. A lower-norm interpolator is a smoother, more stable one — effectively more regularized. So overparametrization, counterintuitively, calms the variance down. The minimum-norm criterion is doing the regularizing implicitly, for free.

The spike at \(\gamma = 1\) is where these forces are both at their worst. With \(p \approx n\), the model has just barely enough capacity to interpolate and no slack to do it gracefully: \(X^\top X\) is nearly singular, the fit threads every noisy point through a single contortion, and the variance term \(\sigma^2/(\gamma-1)\) explodes. The interpolation threshold is not a sweet spot — it is the worst possible place to be.

It’s mostly a regularization story

Here is where I will be opinionated. The dramatic part of the double-descent curve — the divergence at \(\gamma = 1\) — is an artifact of insisting on interpolation. And interpolation is a choice, not a law. Ridgeless least squares is just ridge regression with the penalty turned all the way down to zero, which is precisely the setting a statistician would never choose on purpose.

Hastie et al. (2022) make the point rigorously: optimally-tuned ridge regression dominates minimum-norm least squares at every value of \(\gamma\) and every SNR, in both well-specified and misspecified settings. The optimal ridge risk has no spike — tuning \(\lambda\) properly smooths the entire curve into a single well-behaved descent, and for misspecified models its global minimum sits right around \(\gamma = 1\), exactly where ridgeless regression is at its worst. The good news for practice is that this optimal penalty is estimable: leave-one-out cross-validation recovers it asymptotically.

So my reading is this. Double descent is real and the mathematics is genuinely beautiful, but the headline — more parameters fix overfitting — gets the moral backwards. What the second descent really shows is that the minimum-norm interpolator carries a hidden, increasingly strong dose of regularization as \(\gamma\) grows. A statistician does not need to climb out to \(\gamma = 4\) to enjoy that regularization; she can dial it in directly with a ridge penalty and a cross-validation loop, and never visit the peak at all. The phenomenon is less “interpolation is secretly fine” and more “under-regularization is bad, and overparametrization happens to re-regularize you on the way out.”

When overparametrization genuinely helps

That said, the strong version of the claim — that the global minimum of the risk can live deep in the overparametrized regime — is true, but only under structure, and it is worth knowing which structure.

In the isotropic, well-specified case it essentially never pays: even when \(\text{SNR} > 1\) produces a local minimum for \(\gamma > 1\), the global minimum stays in the underparametrized regime, and the risk only ever approaches the null risk \(r^2\) from below as \(\gamma \to \infty\). Overparametrization buys you nothing you could not get more cheaply.

Two ingredients change the verdict. The first is misspecification: if the true regression function has components your features cannot represent, then adding features genuinely improves the approximation, and with enough signal the global minimum can move past the interpolation boundary. The second is structure in the covariance: when \(\beta\) aligns with the leading eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\) — the “latent space” model, where each new feature carries fresh information about a low-dimensional set of latent drivers — the risk can decrease monotonically across the whole overparametrized regime and reach its minimum as \(\gamma \to \infty\). This is the case that genuinely mirrors what people see in large networks, and it is no accident: the linear model with a learned, structured feature map is exactly the lazy-training (“neural tangent kernel”) linearization of an overparametrized network. The linear story is not an analogy — it is the same story.

Bottom Line

  • Double descent needs no neural networks. It appears in plain ridgeless least squares: out-of-sample risk follows the classical U for \(\gamma = p/n < 1\), diverges at the interpolation boundary \(\gamma = 1\), then descends a second time for \(\gamma > 1\).
  • The mechanics are pure bias–variance. Past the boundary, bias rises (min-norm \(\hat\beta\) is trapped in the \(n\)-dimensional row space) while variance falls (the min-norm interpolant gets smoother as \(p\) grows). The second descent is their tug-of-war.
  • The peak at \(\gamma = 1\) is the worst place to be, not a sweet spot — \(X^\top X\) is near-singular and variance explodes. It is an artifact of forcing interpolation.
  • It is largely a regularization story. Optimally-tuned ridge dominates ridgeless least squares everywhere and erases the spike; the implicit regularization of the min-norm rule is just doing, clumsily, what a ridge penalty does on purpose. Tune \(\lambda\) (e.g. by LOOCV) and the drama disappears.
  • Overparametrization wins globally only under structure: misspecification, high SNR, or \(\beta\) aligned with the top eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\). Absent that, the classical sweet spot is still hard to beat.

Where to Learn More

The paper behind most of this post is Hastie, Montanari, Rosset, and Tibshirani (2022), which derives the exact asymptotic risk of ridgeless and ridge regression in the proportional regime \(p \asymp n\) and is the definitive statistical treatment of double descent. Belkin, Hsu, Ma, and Mandal (2019) is the paper that named the phenomenon and documented it across neural networks, random forests, and kernel machines — the empirical starting point. For the geometry of why the coefficients are unrecoverable but the predictions survive in the overparametrized regime, see my companion post on linear models when \(p > n\). And for the broader theory of when interpolation can be harmless, Bartlett, Long, Lugosi, and Tsybakov (2020) on “benign overfitting” is the natural next stop.
